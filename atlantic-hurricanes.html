Hurricanes in the Atlantic

An assessment of the factors driving the costs of hurricane damage.

Author

Carmen Hoyt

Published

December 10, 2024

About

Growing up on the Gulf Coast of Florida, my family and I were no stranger to hurricanes. We have lived in the same house for over 20 years, but we have seen an increase in flooding and severe damage in more recent hurricane seasons1. For example, Hurricane Ian (2022) stands out as having been the worst storm to hit our neighborhood during my lifetime, bringing over 7 feet of storm surge into our home.

Climate change is influencing natural disasters across the board, and hurricanes are no exception. While the frequency of storms is not projected to change, they are predicted to become more intense2. Hurricanes “are assigned”intensity” is measured by categories 1 through 5 on the Saffir-Simpson scale, assigned by wind speed3. Storms assigned a category 3 or higher are considered “major hurricanes” and are expected to bring catastrophic damage (and thus more significant damage costs).

As storms increase in intensity, are damage costs also being driven by time?

Data

I will be using Kaggle’s North American Hurricanes from 2000 dataset to develop a model of the factors driving damage costs.

I predict that time has an effect on damage costs (increasing over time).

  • H0: Time has no effect on damage costs.
  • HA: Time has an effect on damage costs.

Conclusions

I found that, of my original considerations for predictor variables, max wind speed, rainfall, and time were the best predictors for damage costs. The number of places affected did not prove to be beneficial to add to the model.

I also found that the p-value for Beta 3 (time) was > \(alpha = 0.05\) for both models. Thus, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. Remember:

  • H0: Time has no effect on storm damage costs.

So, to conclude, we could not rule out that the effect of time on damage costs could be due to random chance.

A few factors are not considered in these models:

  • cost of inflation

Additional predictor variables that would be of interest:

  • storm surge
  • water temperature
